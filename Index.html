📖 User Manual

How Discover works

Discover (🧭) is home base for everything your puppy can explore. It's a hub of five journeys — The Story of Earth, Ancient Worlds, Living Earth, Modern Earth, and Space Explorer — plus your Puppy Discoveries. Open it from the compass button on the main screen or from the ⚙️ settings menu.

How to unlock new discoveries

  1. Every step you take (walking) and every kilometer you cycle counts toward the same running total.
  2. Each journey has its own set of stops along the way — an ancient city, a planet, a geological era, an animal fact — unlocked in order as your total grows.
  3. A locked stop shows 🔒 with a hint; once you reach its step count, it unlocks and shows its full story.
  4. Your Puppy Discoveries work the same way — walking further reveals new treasures your puppy finds.
  5. Nothing ever locks back up, and nothing needs an internet connection — everything unlocked stays unlocked.

How walking and cycling connect to exploration

Your phone's motion sensor (or Apple Health import) counts your walking steps, and the 🚴 cycling toggle tracks ride distance separately. Both feed into the very same progress used across every Discover section — so a walk to the shops and a weekend bike ride both push your puppy further along every journey at once.

Prefer not to use motion tracking? Tap "I'll tap instead" during setup, or add steps manually any time from the ⚙️ settings menu — Discover unlocks the same way either way.

🍏 Apple Health Manual

Step-by-step setup

  1. Open the Shortcuts app on your iPhone (it comes pre-installed with iOS).
  2. Create a new shortcut that reads your step count from the Health app.
  3. Add an action to send that number to this app using the "Add steps from Health" option in the ⚙️ settings menu.
  4. Run the shortcut whenever you'd like to bring your latest steps in — no account or sign-in needed.

Permissions

The first time your shortcut reads step data, iOS will ask for permission to access Health data. Choose "Allow" (or "Turn On All") so the shortcut can read your step count. This app itself never asks for or receives direct HealthKit access — it only ever sees the number your shortcut sends it.

Auto Sync

Normally, when your shortcut hands off step data this app asks "Update your puppy?" before applying it. Turn Auto Sync on to skip that confirmation and apply Health data the instant it arrives — handy if you run the shortcut often and trust it.

🔄 Auto Sync: Off

Troubleshooting

  • Nothing happens when I run the shortcut: double-check the shortcut has permission under iPhone Settings → Health → Data Access & Devices → Shortcuts.
  • Steps look too low or too high: Health step counts can include data from multiple devices (phone + watch). Check which sources your Health app is combining under Health → Steps → Data Sources.
  • Progress didn't save: use "Save / Restore data" in settings regularly, since this app stores your progress on this device (and, if configured, its own private backend) rather than inside the Health app.

FAQ

Does this app track my location?
No. Steps and cycling distance are the only inputs — there's no GPS or real-time location tracking involved.
Can I use this without Apple Health at all?
Yes — motion-based auto counting and manual step entry both work with no Health access at all.
Will this drain my battery?
The app is built to be battery-friendly and only keeps the screen awake while actively tracking a walk or ride.

🪜 Ascent Ladder
🚀 Space / Cosmic
Stage 1 · Near Earth
Where you're standing — and just above it
8.8 km (Everest) → 408 km (ISS)
Earth is 12,742 km across — even Everest, the tallest point on the planet, is a scratch on the surface. Keep going up past cruise altitude and the Kármán line (100 km, the recognized edge of space) and you'll reach the ISS — the farthest most humans have ever actually been.
Stage 2 · Earth–Moon System
The farthest people have gone
384,400 km average
You could line up about 30 Earths in the gap between Earth and the Moon. Apollo 13's crew hold the record for humanity's farthest trip — and it barely leaves the neighborhood.
Stage 3 · The Solar System
Earth is one dot among many
1 AU (Earth) → 172 AU (Voyager 1)
Voyager 1, launched in 1977, is our farthest-traveled machine — already past every planet, still not free of the Sun's gravity, and about to reach one light-day away in late 2026.
Stage 4 · The Oort Cloud
Meeting the Oort Cloud
~2,000 → 100,000+ AU (theorized)
The Oort Cloud is a vast, theorized shell of icy debris — never directly observed — that marks the true edge of the Sun's gravity. Voyager 1 won't reach its inner edge for about 300 years, and would take tens of thousands more to cross all the way through it.
Stage 5 · Interstellar Space
Between the stars
4.24 light-years
That's the distance to the nearest known star. Voyager 1, our fastest-traveled object, would still take over 70,000 years to cross that gap.
Stage 6 · The Milky Way
One galaxy, 100,000 light-years wide
100,000–200,000 light-years across
Our entire Solar System is one point partway out on a spiral arm, among 100–400 billion other stars. Light itself needs 100,000 years to cross this galaxy.
Stage 7 · The Local Group
Not alone out here
~10 million light-years across
The Milky Way shares this neighborhood with Andromeda — our closest large galactic neighbor — plus dozens of smaller galaxies. Andromeda and the Milky Way are slowly falling toward each other, on a collision course roughly 4.5 billion years from now.
Stage 8 · The Observable Universe
As far as anyone can ever see
~93 billion light-years across (observable)
This is the edge of what light has had time to reach us since the Big Bang — not the edge of everything that exists. The universe itself may be far larger, possibly infinite, and we may never know for sure.
